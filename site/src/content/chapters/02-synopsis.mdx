The story of early civilization that we learned at school was simple: around 4,000 B.C. civilization began in Babylonia and spread to Egypt, Greece and Rome. Cuneiform and heiroglyphs led to alphabets, which were spread by the Phoenicians (c. 1500 B.C.). This book makes two contributions. First, it examines Sumerian, Indian and Slavic precedents: how early Egyptian and Hebrew alphabets copied Indian alphabets; how the Slavic tradition developed pre-Cyrillic alphabets and a much earlier priestly alphabet. As its main focus, the book explores how letters for speaking, reading, and writing become linked with yoga, cosmology, religion, astronomy, chronology and alchemy.

An opening chapter traces key themes of Slavic alphabets in pre-history, which focus on energies and forces of creation, life, reproduction, eternal life. A second chapter outlines how Sanskrit linked sounds in the mouth (proto-phonology) with elements of creation (4<sup>th</sup> -2<sup>nd</sup> millenium B.C.). In India, the alphabet becomes a story of creation and a reunion with the Divine. Chapters three and four explore how runes and alphabets become linked with yoga and martial arts: movements of breath and movements of the body, which lead to systems of matrices for runes and letters. Subsets of these matrices become Odin’s twigs, Scandinavian stavs, and Anglo-Saxon glory-twigs.

Chapter five explores how letters in alphabets also become starting points for cosmology and sacred structures. 3 key letters are linked with the 3 worlds. 7 letters become linked with 7 planets and 7 chakras in the body (microcosm). 12 letters become linked with 12 zodiac signs, stations of the sun (macrocosm). Chapter six explores how these heavenly models lead to earthly reflections: how Sanskrit leads to sacred geography and sacred chronology and how alphabets become linked with cardinal, fixed, mutable signs to reflect world, year and man.

Chapters seven and eight explore how the Sanskrit model changes as it moves eastwards and westwards. Chapter nine focusses on the structure of Western alphabets. Some use a subset of the Sanskrit sequence (Avestan, Aryan). Others change the sequence (Sumerian, Akkadian, Ugaritic, Phoenician). What begins as a method for explaining the past (abjad) becomes a new method for organizing letters and the cosmos that begins in Sumeria and becomes a paradigm. Hence, Western alphabets have their roots in Sanskrit via Middle Asia.

Chapter ten examines classes of alphabets, offering a new framework for understanding Western alphabets in terms of a) positions of vowels and b) four alphabet models, namely ABGD of 22 letter alphabets (e.g. Hebrew); ABTθ (e.g.Persian, Arabic), ABVD (e.g.Cyrillic) and ABCD (English). Chapter eleven explores how number symbolism (gematria) links letters, numbers, geometry and games. Chapter twelve offers conclusions. Appendices explore signs, stories of the sky, systems in the sky, weaving and decoration, figure-ground, evolution of alphabet trees, abjads, routes of transmission, Chaldea, Africa, and number symbolism.

This book explores how runes and alphabets begin as alphabets of life (alpha vitae), reflecting body, mind and spirit (thought, word, and deed). They link mortal beings with three worlds, and a life eternal expressed in religion, philosophy and literature. These non-objective worlds beyond physical objects unleash dreams, horizons, universes of hope, vision, imagination. They point to a new history of alphabets with histories of individual letters.
