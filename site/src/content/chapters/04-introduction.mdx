- **Hieroglyphs and Cypher Alphabets **

The Egyptians have their own account of the the history of alphabets. According to Ibn Washiyah (9<sup>th</sup> c. A.D.),<Endnote n={24}>See: Hammer, Ancient Alphabets, London, 1806.</Endnote> there were three main dynastries: the Hermesians, the Pharaohs and the Coptic or properly the Egyptian Kings. In terms of modern chronology, this means that the Hermesians were prior to 3,200 B.C.<Endnote n={25}>List of Pharaohs: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List\_of\_pharaohs</Endnote> and had four classes: 1. Hermes the Great, Edris (Enoch); 2. sons of the brother of Hermes, Asclabianos; 3. an Eastern group, children of the sister of Hermes,<Endnote n={26}>Known in Greece as Trismegistos Thoosdios, cf. Hermes Trismegistos.</Endnote> and 4. walkers or peripatetic philosophers, who mingled with the children and family of Hermes.

The Hermesian language (Egyptian hieroglyphs), depicted words or ideas in four classes: a. c[elestial or supernatural objects](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=330721); b. a[nimal actions and affections](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=330722); c. t[rees, plants and their produce](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=330723) and d. m[inerals](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=330724). These four classes established basic distinctions between animal, vegetable, mineral, and were starting points for four modern disciplines: astronomy, biology, botany and mineralogy. Hermesian was linked with the monuments in Upper Egypt which were built: “for the purpose of hiding treasures, of raising spirits, of telling fortunes and future events, of performing chemical operations, of attracting affection, of repelling evils, or of indicating approaching enemies.”<Endnote n={27}>Hammer 1806 x:and they call them according to these supposed purposes, treasure chambers, conjuring buildings, astrological tables, alchemical monuments, magical spells, talismans, and magic alarm posts.</Endnote> Hence, the Hermesian alphabet was not concerned with everyday communication. It was secret, devoted to philosophical, magical and chemical sciences.

The Egyptians called their writing, Medu Neter (Tongue of God or God’s Words). The Greeks called it hieroglyphikos. It was a “system of standardized pictographs that represent both meanings and consonal sounds.”<Endnote n={28}>Writing: http://www.taneter.org/writing.html</Endnote> Their purpose was clearly sacred. But in the period from 3,300 to 600 B.C. the functions of the scripts expanded to recording oil and linen deliveries, historical texts, [meta-physical/spiritual "Pyramid texts,"](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=358227) [coffin texts](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=358229) and [scientific, spiritual and administrative papyri.](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=358225)They also developed Hieratic script, which began as priestly/sacerdotal writing, became used for administrative and scientific documents and had links to Proto-Saharan or Thinite writing.<Endnote n={29}>Writing: http://www.taneter.org/writing.html</Endnote>

According to Ibn Washiya, who presents a Euro-centric view:

The original alphabets from which all other ancient and modern have been derived are no more than three:

1. The Old Syrian alphabet or the first original divine alphabet taught by God the Almighty to Adam

2. The Celestial Alphabet or the alphabet in which the books of Seth (health be with him) received from heaven were written

3. The alphabet of Enoch brought down by the angel Gabriel.<Endnote n={30}>Hammer, 1806, 41-42</Endnote>

Ibn Washiya’s account is of interest here for four reasons. First, the history of alphabets is preceded by a pre-history of cypher-alphabets, devoted to magic, the supernatural and the secret. While the earliest alphabets typically refer to divine origins, they also mark a fundamental shift from secretive knowledge to knowledge, ideas, words, and letters that can be shared and communicated.

Second, the names Adam, Seth and Enoch, linked with the three antidiluvian alphabets, clearly have pre-Christian roots. Third, at least two of the seven most famous ancient languages in the West (Berrabian and Lukumian) are all but unknown today. Hence, any attempt at a comprehensive history of alphabets will prove elusive. Fourth, it is noteworthy that Indian (of three different sorts) was one of the three main alphabets used in Egypt, while Davidian, one of the 24 cypher alphabets, “was particularly used in India by many learned men in their writing on medicine, philosophy and politics. It was one of the most celebrated alphabets.”<Endnote n={36}>Hammer 1806, p. 8.</Endnote> These ancient connections between India and Egypt are the more significant because the Numeral or Indian Alphabet has a sequence known in the West as AIQ BEKAR (i.e. letter sequence 1 10 19; 2 11 20, figure 118). The same sequence is found in the primeval alphabet Mimshim (figure i, cf. figure 236), in the Hebrew Cabala and derives from the system of Indian mansions of the moon (figure 505 and table 100). As will be shown in the coming chapters, there are numerous unexpected links between East and West.

- ** Stages of Alphabets **

Earlier scholars have distinguished stages in the evolution of writing systems. For instance, Diringer,<Endnote n={37}>Diringer, 1948, pp. 21-37.</Endnote> identifies four kinds of writing which serve to mark four stages of development: 1. embryo-writing; 2. picture writing; 3. ideographic writing and 4. phonetic writing. Stage one, embro-writing entails i[conography, sympathetic magic](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=355554), [m](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=355566)nemonic devices, symbolic means of communication and occurs as [animated objects,](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=355563)[coloured pebbles](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=355561), c[onventionalized figures](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=355557), g[eometric marks, signs, symbols; n](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=355559)[umerical indications](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=355562) and especially as rock pictures (petroglyphs).<Endnote n={38}>For an excellent introduction to this pre-history of writing see: Földes-Papp, Karoly: Vom Felsbild zum Alphabet Die Geschichte der Schrift von ihren frühesten Vorstufen bis zur modernen lateinischen Schreibschrift, Stuutgart: Belser Verlag, 1984. (ISBN: 3763016422 / 3-7630-1642-2).</Endnote> This may include hundreds or even thousands of glyphs (marks, signs, symbols), but no clear system or numeration.

Rock art evolved into three forms: “[petroglyphs](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroglyphs) which are carved into the rock surface, [pictographs](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pictographs) which are painted onto the surface, and [earth figures](http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Earth_figures&action=edit&redlink=1) [geoglyphs] engraved into the ground.”<Endnote n={39}>Rock Art: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock\_art</Endnote> Pictographs (pictograms)<Endnote n={40}>Wiki classes these as: Pictographic/ideographic writing systems</Endnote> become an initial kind of Chinese writing: [xiangxing 象形 "illustrating a shape."](http://new.sumscorp.com/index.php?id=249&statement=get_obj&id_object=333080)In Diringer’s model, pictograms become stage 2: picture writing (pictographic writing): e.g. cuneiform and hieroglyphic writing. This stage typically has thousands of glyphs and some initial ordering, such as the four classes of hieroglyphs.

Stage 3 in Diringer’s model is ideographic writing, also called logographic writing by others. Strictly speaking, this is the third type of Chinese character: xiangyi 象意 "illustrating an idea", in Chinese. Diringer includes the whole of Chinese as “ideographic or rather ‘transitional’ writing.”<Endnote n={41}>Diringer, 1948, p.98.</Endnote> Hence, all six types of Chinese characters are subsumed in stage 3: illustrating a shape, an affair, an idea, a sound, mutual comment and wrongly borrowing.<Endnote n={42}>Chinese Characters: http://www.chinaknowledge.de/Literature/Script/hanzi.htmlxiangxing 象形 "illustrating a shape", pictographicxiangshi 象事 "illustrating an affair",xiangyi 象意 "illustrating an idea", ideographicxiangsheng 象聲 "illustrating a sound", phonetic zhuanzhu 轉注 "mutual comment" and jiajie 假借 "wrongly borrowing".</Endnote> Although the Chinese system has only 214 radicals, it generates as many as 106,230 characters. Only 3,300 characters are needed for 99% comprehension of modern texts. Not discussed by Diringer is pasigraphy (coined 1797), “a [writing system](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing_system) where each written symbol represents a concept (rather than a word or sound or series of sounds in a spoken language).”<Endnote n={43}>Pasigraphy: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pasigraphy</Endnote> Early examples included Leibnitz’s alphabet of human thought (characteristica universalis). More recent efforts are linked with efforts for an International auxiliary language, a universal language and a universal alphabet.

Stage 4 in Diringer’s evolution is phonetic writing. In the Chinese character types, this is: xiangsheng 象聲 "illustrating a sound."<Endnote n={44}>Hanzi: http://www.chinaknowledge.de/Literature/Script/hanzi.htmlCf. Diringer, 1948, 114: Hsing Sheng, formulate. harmonize sound: Phonetic Compounds.</Endnote> In Diringer’s scheme, this is subdivided into a) syllabic writing and b) alphabetic writing. Syllabic writing systems typically combined vowels along one axis and consonants along another axis to create grids ranging from as few as 45 (3 vowels x 15 consonants) to 1,296 signs (e.g. 36 x 36 biconsonantal signs). For Diringer, stages 1, 2, 3, and 4a comprise non-alphabetic systems of writing, to which he dedicates part 1 of his book.<Endnote n={45}>Diringer uses the the term Transitional scripts to cover stages 2, 3 and 4a.</Endnote> Part 2 of his book is devoted to stage 4b: alphabetic writing.

These distinctions reveal how the “invention” of the alphabet in its modern sense, entailed an enormous narrowing in scope of signs. In terms of the six types of Chinese characters, five were abandoned: only illustrating a sound, phonetics and phonology remained. They also help in clarifying the scope of present work. Magical and cipher alphabets, various stages of writing, the complex transition from signs expressive of pictures (pictograms) words and ideas (hieroglyphs, pictograms, ideograms) to the signification of individual letters, are largely beyond the scope of our study, although some key examples<Endnote n={46}>Hammer 1806 p. viiiMimshim “shows a transition of hieroglyphics from being signs expressive of words to the signification of simple letters.”</Endnote> will be mentioned. Non-alphabetic systems will be considered mainly in chapter 1 and appendix 1.

Our focus is on alphabetic systems. Whereas Diringer started in the near East to claim that Aramaic moved eastwards across Iran to India and was also the basis of languages in further India: Indo-China, Malaya, Indonesia and the Philippines, our story will suggest that Indian models moved both eastwards to the Pacific and Westwards to Africa, and that there is an older story in pre-history, where proto-Slavic karuna, runes, and letters moved both North-South and East-West. In the current view, a Semitic source leads to the world alphabets. In our story, China evolves one solution. Aryan sources produce Slavic and Sanskrit, subsets of which become Latin and Arabic, the two most widespread international writing systems and alphabets.

**Method**

Modern books typically use a single standard name for alphabets. For the purposes of this study which explores the history of variants within alphabets, such a convention would add more confusion than clarity, because it blurs distinctions between the various versions. Hence, we use the standard name when referring to the modern form and retain alternative names from sources for earlier versions. Hence, there are references to Aethiopisch, Ethiopien, Eythyiopique, when referring historical examples of Ethiopian. The numbering of different versions of alphabets by Fry (1799) is also used: e.g. Chaldean 1-17.
